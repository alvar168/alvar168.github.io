Antonio Alvarez Valdivia

I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Mechanical Engineering at Purdue University, specializing in soft robotics, haptics and human-machine interaction. I am currently a Research Assistant in the RAAD Lab, working under Prof. Laura Blumenschein.

My work focuses on designing innovative soft haptic interfaces and shape-changing displays for applications in human-robot and human-machine interaction. My expertise spans soft robotics, haptics, mechatronics, and human factors in engineering.

I am actively seeking full-time opportunities to apply my skills and contribute to advancing human-centered technologies after my graduation in July 2025.

Projects

Wrapped Haptic Display to Communicate Robot Learning

Soft, wrapped haptic displays that are mounted on the robot arm at the point of contact between the human and robot; these displays provide real-time feedback as the robot learns from human demonstrations.

Inflatable Shape-Changing Interfaces

Inspired by recent developments on shape-changing pin displays and soft growing robots, we design a flexible growing pin for shape-changing inflatable displays with high extension and passive retraction. Each pin consists of a rigid, air-tight chamber, an inflatable fabric pin, and a passive spring-actuated reel mechanism.

Control of Pneumatically Actuated Interfaces with Soft Fluidic Logic

This project focuses on simplifying control of pneumatically actuated haptic interfaces by using soft fluidic logic systems. These systems incorporate soft bistable valves (developed by Rothemund et al., Science Robotics) arranged to create programmable logic for controlling multiple outputs with limited pneumatic inputs. The design enables the generation of inflation patterns with a single triggering signal. By eliminating the need for traditional electro-pneumatic equipment, this approach streamlines the control of multi-degree of freedom systems, making them more accessible and scalable.

Signal Complexity Effects on the Task-based Utility of Haptic Information

Investigation on the differences in human perception and human use of sensory feedback in navigation-related experiments. We use an inflatable soft haptic display configured to operate in different spatial encoding complexities. By comparing these sets of haptic signals, both in static and reactive perception scenarios, we seek to understand the differences between human perception of and human use of sensory feedback as it relates to signal complexity.

Micro and Mili-fluidics

During my undergraduate education at Iowa State University, I worked on multiple projects for Prof. Jaime Juarez. These projects focused on microrheology, colloidal science, microfluidics, and milifluidics. Specifically, I worked on portable imaging viscometry devices, flexible circuits with ferrofluids, self-propelled PDMS floaters, and acoustic manipulations and assembly of particles.

Soft Sensors

This project focused on enhancing soft robotic gripping systems by integrating flexible force sensors to provide real-time feedback for adjusting adhesion levels between the gripper and objects. A parallel-plate capacitor architecture was was employed to fabricate 10x10mm and 25x25mm sensors using PDMS or Ecoflex as the dielectric layer and copper film electrodes on elastomer substrates. Capacitance changes from 1-20pF were achieved, with thicknesses of the dielectric layer varying from 400μm to 1.2mm. Multiple electronic measuring systems, including a 555-timer circuit and capacitance-to-digital converter, were explored for interpreting capacitance changes.
